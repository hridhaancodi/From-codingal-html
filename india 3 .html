About India

Unity in diversity is the principle that harmony and cohesion can exist among people or groups despite differences in culture, religion, language, or ideology.

India is a vast South Asian country, the world’s most populous democracy, known for its rich history, diverse culture, and rapid socio-economic growth.

About Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal is a 17th-century ivory-white marble mausoleum in Agra, India, built by Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his wife Mumtaz Mahal.

About Gateway of India

The Gateway of India is a monumental arch in Mumbai, built to commemorate the visit of King George V and Queen Mary in 1911, symbolizing India's colonial past and independence.

Taj Palace Hotel

The Taj Mahal Palace, Mumbai, a heritage luxury hotel located in the Colaba area, is renowned for its stunning views of the Arabian Sea and the Gateway of India. Opened in 1903, it is one of the most iconic hotels in India

About East India

East India is a culturally rich and historically significant region of India, comprising the states of Bihar, Jharkhand, Odisha, and West Bengal, along with the Andaman and Nicobar Islands.

Mahabodhi Temple

The Mahabodhi Temple Complex is one of the four holy sites related to the life of the Lord Buddha, and particularly to the attainment of Enlightenment. The first temple was built by Emperor Asoka in the 3rd century B.C., and the present temple dates from the 5th or 6th centuries. It is one of the earliest Buddhist temples built entirely in brick, still standing in India, from the late Gupta period.

North India is a geographical and cultural region in India that includes two union territories (Delhi and Chandigarh) and seven states (Haryana, Punjab, Uttar Pradesh, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Jammu & Kashmir, and Himachal Pradesh), covering about 44% of the country's land area indovacations.net indovacations.net . This region is characterized by its diverse landscapes, ranging from the majestic Himalayas to the fertile plains of the Ganges, and is known for its rich history, culture, and traditions

Agra Fort

Agra Fort, also known as the Red Fort, is a historical Mughal fort located in Agra, Uttar Pradesh, India. It was built by Emperor Akbar in 1565 and served as the royal residence for four generations of the Mughal dynasty. The fort is renowned for its architectural marvels, including the Moti Masjid, Diwan-i-Aam, Diwan-i-Khas, and Musamman Burj, where Emperor Shah Jahan had died in imprisonment

About West India

West India is a vibrant region known for its diverse geography, rich cultural heritage, and significant historical importance, comprising states like Maharashtra, Gujarat, Goa, and the union territories of Daman and Diu.

Elephanta Caves

The Elephanta Caves are located in Western India on Elephanta Island (otherwise known as the Island of Gharapuri), which features two hillocks separated by a narrow valley. The small island is dotted with numerous ancient archaeological remains that are the sole testimonies to its rich cultural past. These archaeological remains reveal evidence of occupation from as early as the 2nd century BC. The rock-cut Elephanta Caves were constructed about the mid-5th to 6th centuries AD. The most important among the caves is the great Cave 1, which measures 39 metres from the front entrance to the back. In plan, this cave in the western hill closely resembles Dumar Lena cave at Ellora, in India. The main body of the cave, excluding the porticos on the three open sides and the back aisle, is 27 metres square and is supported by rows of six columns each.

About south India

South India, also known as Southern India or Peninsular India, is the southern part of the Deccan Peninsula in India. It encompasses the states of Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Telangana, as well as the union territories of Lakshadweep and Puducherry. This region occupies 19.31% of India's area and houses about 20% of India's population . South India is known for its rich history, diverse culture, and natural beauty, bordered by the Bay of Bengal to the east, the Arabian Sea to the west, and the Indian Ocean to the south .

Hampi

Hampi or Hampe (Kannada: [hɐmpe]), also referred to as the Group of Monuments at Hampi, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site located in the town of Hampi in Vijayanagara district, east-central Karnataka, India.[2] Hampi predates the Vijayanagara Empire; it is mentioned in the Ramayana and the Puranas of Hinduism as Pampa Devi Tirtha Kshetra.[3][4] Hampi continues as a religious centre, with the Virupaksha Temple, an active Adi Shankara-linked monastery and various monuments belonging to the old city.[5][6] Hampi was the capital of the Vijayanagara Empire from 1336 to 1565 (as Vijayanagara), when it was abandoned.[3] It was a fortified city. Chronicles left by Persian and European travellers, particularly the Portuguese, say that Hampi was a prosperous, wealthy and grand city near the Tungabhadra River, with numerous temples, farms and trading markets. Hampi-Vijayanagara is estimated to be the world's second-largest city by 1500, after Beijing, and probably India's richest at that time, attracting traders from Persia and Portugal.[7][8] The Vijayanagara Empire was defeated by a coalition of Muslim sultanates; its capital was conquered, pillaged and destroyed by Muslim armies in 1565, after which Hampi remained in ruins.
